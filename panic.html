Panic Disorder

A panic attack is a sudden rush of intense anxiety that seems to come out of nowhere. It can happen anytime, even while you’re asleep.
If you have them regularly and are very afraid of having another attack, you could have panic disorder.

Signs

Many of the same symptoms that accompany general anxiety such as a racing heart or pain in your stomach happen with a panic attack.

However, panic attacks are more intense, build quickly and then subside.

 
Other symptoms include trembling, feeling like you can't breathe, being afraid you're going to die, a sense that you're going crazy.

What can you do when you have a panic attack?

Breathe

If you feel an onset of a panic attack, start focusing on your breathing.

To slow down your body’s reaction to anxiety, practise some deep breathing. 

If you are not sure how to do proper breathing, there are phone apps and meditation videos available that can help you through.

Get to a quiet place

You may not know when you may have a panic attack as it can happen when you least expect it.

When you’re in the middle of a panic attack, the last thing you want is more stimuli.

So if possible, move to a quieter space where you can focus on relaxation techniques. This doesn’t mean avoiding situations altogether. It means giving yourself some time to regroup before returning.

It might be as simple as stepping into the bathroom for a few minutes to do some deep breathing.

Know your physical health status

Go to the doctor for regular check ups to know more about your physical health.

Knowing more about your physical health puts you in a better position to tell the difference between panic attack symptoms and signs of a medical condition.

Also, going for regular checkups can help you rule out other conditions that would be cause for alarm.

Think about the specific symptoms you’re experiencing. Are they things common during a panic attack? Inform your doctor about the symptoms and learn what they mean.

Practice healthy habits

Practice healthy habits such as eating well, getting enough rest and regular exercise.

These habits are helpful with stress reduction overall.

Alcohol, cigarettes, and caffeine can also be triggers for your attacks. So, cutting them out of your lifestyle can help reduce these panic attacks.

If panic attacks are keeping you from living the life you want, don’t be afraid to ask for help. A therapist will be able to help you identify triggers and give you tools to prevent and manage future attacks.

The 5-4-3-2-1 Method

One common grounding technique is the 5-4-3-2-1 exercise. Grounding techniques are recommended to prevent anxiety from increasing and interfering with daily life. As you feel your body becoming anxious, you can do this exercise to reduce the “spacey” feelings and prevent the spiral downward to a panic attack

Acknowledge 5 THINGS around you that you can SEE.

Maybe it is a clock on the wall, carpet on the floor, a table or chair, clouds moving past, or a nearby tree. However big or small, recognize 5 items you can see with your eyes.

Acknowledge 4 THINGS around you that you can TOUCH.

Maybe it is your computer at work, the chair you are sitting on, your legs or hair, or your wallet or purse. Recognize 4 items you can feel with your hands or body.

Acknowledge 3 THINGS around you that you can HEAR.

Maybe it is the sound of people talking or walking, the laughter of children, birds chirping. Use your fine tuning and see if you can hear ambient sounds you may not normally tune into – the hum of fans or the AC, clocks ticking, cars going by. Name 3 things that are audible to you.

Acknowledge 2 THINGS around you that you can SMELL.

This one may be tricky if you are not in a stimulating environment, so if you cannot automatically sniff something out, walk nearby to find a scent. You might walk to a bathroom to smell soap, outside to smell something in nature, lean over and smell a pillow on the couch, a pencil on the desk, or check to see how your deodorant is working today. Whatever it may be, take in the smells around you.

Acknowledge 1 THING that you can TASTE.

It might be the aftertaste of coffee, gum or your last meal. Or take a sip of water or grab a snack if it is handy. We also recommend taking an additional deep breath when done

At the end of the exercise, celebrate your success! Recognize you were able to ground yourself and prevent the anxiety spiral, which will help you remember that coping with anxiety is possible and that you have successfully completed this exercise. The next time you feel anxiety returning, remind yourself of previous success.
