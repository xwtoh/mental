Helping someone

Don’t scold someone with mental illness or tell them to stop performing rituals.

When they can’t comply- the pressure to stop will only make the behaviours worse. Be as kind and patient as possible. Each sufferer needs to overcome problems at their own pace.

If you notice the warning signs and symptoms of a mental illness in a friend or family, it’s important to speak up.

You may be afraid that you’re mistaken, or that you’ll say the wrong thing, or you might alienate the person. However, it’s important that you don’t let these worries stop you from voicing your concerns.

Some are struggling just as much as you are to find a way to start a conversation about their problem, while others have such low self-esteem they simply don’t feel that they deserve any help. Whatever the case, eating disorders will only get worse without treatment, and the physical and emotional damage can be severe. The sooner you start to help, the better their chances of recovery. 

Most importantly, avoid using insults to try and jolt them out of how they’re feeling. Insults are guaranteed not to work and may even worsen the problem.

They may find this process embarrassing or difficult, but reassure them that professional help is the most effective way to treat mental illness.

Offer to help them find the right kind of support, and you may perhaps accompany them to their first appointment, if they decide to meet with a specialist.

People trained specifically to help those with mental illness(es) can offer unique treatment strategies based on individual circumstances.

What to do when your loved one/friend doesn’t want help?

Making the decision to get help can be really difficult. If your friend or loved one is finding it hard to accept your offer of help, don’t take it personally.

Don’t try to guilt-trip/ pressure the person into feeling bad about their behaviour by focusing on how it’s affecting other people.

A person with a mental illness is probably already feeling bad, so, focusing on the impact they’re having on others is likely to make them withdraw and feel worse.

Continue to be supportive.

Listen to your friend when he/she needs to talk.

Give suggestions,

if and when your friend reaches out to you and asks for your advice.

Become informed by doing a bit of research into what help is available that could be useful for your friend.

When your friend decides to seek help, you will be ready to give them resources and direction on who to go and see.

Talk to someone you can trust and and share how you are feeling.

Remember to look after yourself too when you’re looking after someone else.

Set boundaries.

You’re not going to be able to be there for someone at every moment of every day. Set some limits on things you’re willing and not willing to do – and stick to them! 

Don’t avoid them.

If you avoid your friend, it’s likely to make them feel isolated. It may also mean that if when they are ready to seek help, they might not feel comfortable about coming to you for support.

If things are really serious, seek help immediately!

While, in most circumstances, it's a good idea to give a friend time to come around to the idea of seeking help, if you think someone is in danger or is at risk as a result of what’s going on, it’s important that you seek help immediately.
